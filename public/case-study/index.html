§1 · The Strategic Bet

Stop designing cars.
Start designing a platform.

1
Re-architect the HMI from a per-vehicle deliverable into a scalable platform — modular, reflowable, vehicle-agnostic.
2
De-risk early. I brought Engineering and the Vehicle Program in up front to pressure-test Air's architecture and Gravity's feasibility — so by UX 2.0's decision points, Dev and Vehicle were already aligned.

A per-vehicle deliverable  →  one scalable platform.

§1 · #1 Planning & Strategy
The bet I made: stop shipping a car's HMI and start shipping a platform — modular, reflowable, vehicle-agnostic. And I de-risked it early — I pulled Engineering and Vehicle Program in before we committed, so when we hit decision points there were no surprises and no blockers.

Visual design refresh for Gravity.

Built on Lucid's brand identity and Air's existing visual style — refreshed into four design principles.

§4 · #4 Design Mastery
§4 opener. The visual refresh wasn't invented from scratch — it's built on two sources: Lucid's overall brand identity, and Air's existing visual style. From those, I refreshed four design principles that guided every Gravity surface. [Click through each, briefly] Safety — frictionless touch targets, glanceable information. Modernity — simple iconography, bold and surprising layouts. Materiality — translucency and light, a subtle ethereal 3D space. Luxe — sharp, high-quality aesthetics that feel expensive and crafted. Each principle a deliberate move forward from where Air's visual language was, not a discard.

Where Lucid sits in the auto landscape.

Lucid's brand positioning in auto — between futuristic and minimal, distinct from feature creep, old luxury, and emotionless function.

§4 · #4 Design Mastery
Where Lucid's brand sits in the auto landscape. We mapped competitors across two axes — futuristic vs traditional, theatrical vs minimal. Each corner has a failure mode: feature creep and sensory overload in the futuristic-theatrical corner; pure function with no emotion in the futuristic-minimal corner; excess and old luxury in the traditional-theatrical corner. Lucid landed deliberately between futuristic and minimal — modern, clean, premium — but with the warmth that separates us from the cold function quadrant. This map informed every visual decision that followed.

The visual direction we chose.

Five design decisions behind Gravity's new visual language.

  • Visual layer + depth. Express hierarchy through z-axis layering — content sits at different elevations, not flat.
  • Frosted glass. Translucent material across surfaces — modern, refined, and quietly premium.
  • Subtle Light mode. Added a refined Light variant — softer, not a full bright daylight mode.
  • Air heritage. Every choice stays rooted in Air's existing visual style — continuity, not rupture.
  • 3D effects. Dimensional depth layered across surfaces — geometry and light, not decoration.
§4 · #4 Design Mastery
Five design decisions defined Gravity's new visual language. One — visual layer and depth: hierarchy through z-axis layering, content at different elevations, never flat. Two — frosted glass: translucent material across surfaces, modern and quietly premium. Three — subtle Light mode: I added a refined Light variant on top of Dark — softer than a full daylight mode, easier on the eyes during day driving. Four — Air heritage: every visual choice stays rooted in Air's existing style; this was continuity, not rupture. Five — 3D effects: dimensional depth layered into surfaces through geometry and light, not decoration. [Image] shows each decision realized on a concrete surface.

Primary Gold — clearer selection, cleaner system.

One more Air pain point — fixed at the system level.

  • Air's pain. The UI highlight used Brand Gold — selected states blurred against everything else gold-tinted in the cockpit; "what's selected" wasn't instantly clear.
  • Gravity's fix. A new UI Gold — distinct from Brand Gold, but still part of the family — replaced highlights across the entire visual system. State now reads at a glance.
§4 · #4 Design Mastery
This slide isn't just a color study — it's another Air pain point fixed at the system level. Air's UI highlight used the same Brand Gold as the corporate logo color. In practice, this meant selected states blurred against everything else gold-tinted in the cockpit — you couldn't always tell what was "selected" at a glance. Small problem on a single screen, big problem when it cascades across hundreds of surfaces. The fix: I introduced a new UI Gold, distinct from Brand Gold but still part of the gold family, and replaced all highlights across the entire visual system. Suddenly "selected" was instantly readable everywhere — and Brand Gold could stay where it belonged, on the brand. This is what "system-level color decision" actually means: not picking a pretty hue, but solving an ambiguity that propagated through every screen.

A platform decision — 3D engine for parts of the HMI.

Render select HMI functions on a real-time 3D engine — a qualitative shift in both visual style and interaction.

  • Visual lift. Materials, lighting, and depth respond to live state — a leap from flat compositing into real geometry and light.
  • Interaction lift. Parallax, camera moves, and physical-feeling controls — a competitive edge over peers locked to 2D rendering.

§4 · #4 Design Mastery
A platform-level visual design decision. We established that select HMI functions would render on a real-time 3D engine — not faked imagery composed in 2D, but real geometry, real materials, real light. Look at the two surfaces here. Same Driving control, two camera angles, two environments. Sunset, desert. The car itself is live-rendered, the environment around it responds, and the drive modes — including a context-aware TERRAIN option — reflect the situation. Visually — materials, lighting, depth respond to live state rather than being pre-baked. Interactively — camera moves, parallax, and physical-feeling controls make the UI tangible, not screen-deep. At the time, most peers were locked into 2D rendering pipelines — this was a real competitive edge.

§4 · #4 Design Mastery
Rebuilt the design system underneath. Components, type, elevation, states, light and dark — extended from Air's toolkit and scaled across every Lucid program. Two moves mattered most. One — cross-platform unification: global components, unified color tokens, shared foundational resources every team draws from; one design language for everything Lucid ships.
